Grapes and Metabolic Syndrome. Ara Kirakosyan, E. Mitchell Seymour, and Steven F. Bolling.

Abstract. The significance of grapes and grape-derived products as healthy foods is well established. Beyond their delightful taste and flavors, grapes offer numerous medicinal benefits due to a wide spectrum of bioactive compounds. These phytochemicals have been extensively studied for their potential to prevent metabolic syndrome. This chapter discusses the effects of grape polyphenolics in preventing or mitigating metabolic syndrome, highlighting several targets and reviewing their mechanistic effects. Adopting a diet that includes grapes may help address the metabolic clusters associated with this condition. Understanding the interplay between bioactive metabolites, gene expression, and phenotype is crucial for elucidating the complex mechanisms underlying grape-mediated protection against metabolic syndrome. Studies in rodents have shown that consuming grape products impacts multiple factors, including metabolic activity and subsequent metabolic adaptation, in models of metabolic syndrome. Additionally, evidence from clinical studies supports the significant role of grapes in combating metabolic syndrome. This chapter will explore the therapeutic potential of grape polyphenolics in managing metabolic syndrome, potential mechanisms of action, future challenges, and research directions related to the effects of grapes on this condition.

Introduction. Grapes are gaining popularity as a healthful food due to their potential to reduce the risk of metabolic syndrome and its complications, such as type 2 diabetes and cardiovascular diseases. As the public health burden of metabolic syndrome increases, functional foods rich in polyphenolics and other bioactive natural products show significant translational potential. This includes promoting grape consumption for its possible health benefits.

One in ten adults in the USA and worldwide has developed metabolic syndrome. This is characterized by clusters of biochemical events in the body that can lead to various complications and diseases. Metabolic syndrome encompasses phenotypes such as obesity, systemic inflammation, hyperglycemia and insulin resistance, and hyperlipidemia. These conditions significantly elevate the risk of developing type 2 diabetes mellitus and cardiovascular disease. It is not only related to cardiovascular diseases but may also be connected to dysfunctions in the kidneys, liver, muscles, and other organs. While the factors causing metabolic syndrome are well characterized, targeted molecular-level analysis is still underway to elucidate possible gene regulation or enzyme functions. Several genes related to hyperglycemia, hyperlipidemia, obesity, hypertension, and insulin resistance have been identified and studied to date. However, it remains unclear how bioactive metabolites from grapes might help prevent or treat metabolic syndrome through direct intervention or dietary changes.

Research indicates that the regular consumption of fruits may help prevent and manage various risk factors associated with metabolic syndrome and its complications. Adopting optimal dietary habits can be a learned way to prevent or manage type 2 diabetes mellitus, one of the most prevalent noncommunicable diseases. It is noteworthy that the full metabolic profiling, including grape metabolomics data, is not yet fully unraveled. Understanding the diversity of phytochemicals, the predominant polyphenolics, their interactions with other molecules, and potential drug interactions is crucial for guiding dietary recommendations and enhancing therapeutic efficacy. This knowledge can offer new and innovative insights that could influence clinical dietary counseling and public health strategies.

Studying the metabolic fate of grapes with complex phytochemical ingredients is particularly challenging because the exact composition and active molecules are usually unknown and the absorption and metabolism of these phytochemicals in organisms may be highly variable. Research into the bioavailability and bioactivity of grape metabolites in animals and humans is ongoing. Several studies aim to understand how these compounds are absorbed, metabolized, and utilized in the body. While the potential health benefits of grapes and their major bioactive polyphenols, such as resveratrol, have been widely discussed, more research is needed to confirm their efficacy in managing metabolic syndrome. In this context, the interplay between bioactive metabolites, gene expression, and phenotype is essential for unraveling the complex mechanisms underlying grape-mediated protection against metabolic syndrome. Studies in rodents have shown that consuming grape products affects multiple factors, including metabolic activity and subsequent metabolic adaptation, in an animal model of metabolic syndrome. Determining the effects of grape intake on laboratory variables with several known markers, including fasting glucose, insulin, cholesterol, triglycerides, and C-reactive protein, functions including blood pressure, systolic function, and diastolic function, and specific genes related to antioxidant defense and inflammation can illuminate how grape metabolites may help prevent conditions associated with metabolic syndrome. To gain a comprehensive understanding, the next stage involves using advanced statistical and modeling approaches to assess the interactions among the measured variables.

Grape Polyphenolics. Phenolic compounds in grapes can be categorized into four main groups: simple phenols, flavonoids, anthocyanins, and stilbenes. These compounds are often referred to as polyphenols due to their shared phenolic structure. The variation in oxidation levels and hydroxylation patterns of the phenolic rings gives rise to a diverse array of structures, each with distinct biological behaviors, bioavailability, and efficacy. Notably, grape polyphenols, which hold potential medical interest, predominantly reside in the skins, seeds, vine stems, and grape plant leaves.

Polyphenolic compounds in grapes mainly include monomeric and oligomeric flavanols. Flavanols, also known as flavan-3-ols, and their oligomers and polymers, known as proanthocyanidins, are known for their potent antioxidant and anti-inflammatory effects. These polyphenolic compounds are typically divided into two groups: flavonoids and nonflavonoids. Flavonoids possess a unique core structure known as the flavane nucleus, which includes two benzene rings, the A and B rings, connected by a pyran ring containing oxygen. Due to the phenolic group attached to one of the benzene rings, these substances are also referred to as phenols or polyphenols. Variations in the oxidation of the heterocyclic C ring and the hydroxylation of the three rings lead to a diverse family of structures with bioactive properties.

The most common flavonoids in grapes include flavonols such as quercetin, kaempferol, and myricetin; flavan-3-ols including catechin, epicatechin, and tannins; and anthocyanins in red or black grapes. Flavonoids can exist either free or as sugar conjugates known as glycosides, other flavonoids, and nonflavonoids. Significant variations in polyphenol content are observed among the different components of grapes, such as the juice, pomace, and seeds. These differences are also evident across various grape varietals and distinct agricultural regions, reflecting the influence of both genetic and environmental factors. In grape phytochemistry, phenols and polyphenols include larger molecular structures that form oligomeric chains, contributing to the complexity of grape polyphenols.

Grape skins and pomace are abundant in various polyphenols, such as phenolic acids, anthocyanins, flavonols, and polygalloyl polyflavan-3-ols. These compounds are found not only in fresh grapes but also in processed products, like grape juice, wine, and grape skin extracts. Grape seed extract is particularly known for its high concentration of larger-molecular-weight compounds. These compounds are primarily composed of repeating flavan-3-ol units esterified with gallic acid, forming more extensive chains than those typically found in grape skin. Grape seed extract is also rich in proanthocyanidins, a group of high-molecular-weight flavonols. These compounds are present in significantly higher amounts in grape seed extract compared to grape skin, offering a superior profile of health-promoting benefits. During the winemaking process, chemical constituents from grape seeds and stems are often imparted into the wine. This transfer occurs through prolonged contact with macerated grapes during vinification, introducing these complex molecules into the final beverage.

Despite the extensive presence of these compounds in various grape-related products, the specific biological or health effects of individual grape constituents remain a subject of several ongoing studies. It is yet to be determined which components yield the most significant benefits or whether there is a synergistic effect when these compounds interact.

Bioavailability of Grape Bioactive Compounds: A Link Between Tissue Bioavailability and Metabolic Health. The bioavailability of grape secondary metabolites in humans is not well understood. Historically, the main challenges have been the very low concentrations of these compounds and the difficulty in detecting specific compounds in tissues or excretory organs, even with advanced analytical instrumentation. Although there are several reports on bioavailability and their potential links to bioactivities, comprehensive metabolome analysis often fails to identify specific transformations or increases in bioactivity within certain time frames. Biochemical reactions can sometimes be masked, making it difficult to detect final products. However, a reverse approach can illuminate the specificity of particular polyphenolic compounds.

Bioavailability can be described by several known factors and their functional roles. When discussing metabolic syndrome, it is crucial to analyze the link between the types of compounds present in grapes and their bioavailability. This specialized functional metabolomics approach could effectively explain the potential effects of grape metabolites, their bioavailability, and bioactivities that target specific clusters of events in the development of metabolic syndrome.

Bioavailability can be understood in various contexts. When discussing the oral intake of grapes, it generally refers to the fraction of the ingested dose that is absorbed. Specifically, bioavailability is the proportion of the nutrient that is digested, absorbed, and metabolized through normal pathways. Therefore, it is crucial not only to know the quantity of a nutrient present in grapes but also to understand how much of it is bioavailable. Recent studies on bioavailability have shown that some polyphenolics are rapidly absorbed from the stomach and small intestine, appearing in plasma and urine in their original form or as methylated, glucuronidated, or sulfated compounds. The proportion of total flavonoids absorbed and excreted in urine, both in native form and as metabolites, is typically less than one percent of the ingested amount, which is generally accurate for most fruit polyphenolics.

The half-life of plasma polyphenolic constituents and their metabolites typically spans only a few hours. However, emerging research suggests that these constituents may be integrated into body tissues, potentially prolonging their biological impact, which includes the possibility of genetic alteration. Interestingly, many experimental studies using products rich in phytochemicals have shown biological activity even when the original compounds are not significantly present in the bloodstream. Consequently, a growing hypothesis suggests that the primary agents behind the detected biological effects are not the parent compounds themselves but rather their enterohepatic conjugates or colonic metabolites. Phytochemicals are extensively altered during metabolism, so that the molecular forms reaching the peripheral circulation and tissues are different from those present in whole foods. Here, the term metabolism describes the typical modifications that occur during or after absorption, which includes modifications made to intracellular metabolites.

The catabolism of phytochemicals in mammals generally occurs as a result of microbial activity in the large intestine. Recent studies have shown that the gut microbiota plays a crucial role in the biotransformation of these compounds, leading to the production of bioactive metabolites. Most phytochemical glucosides are deglycosylated by beta-glucosidases in the small intestine, including broad-specificity cytosolic beta-glucosidase and lactase phlorizin hydrolase. This step is essential for the absorption of many of these phytochemicals. Polyphenols and their derivatives are further metabolized in the liver by phase two enzymes, undergoing glucuronidation and sulfation to form conjugates, which can be methylated at the catechol functional group. Many of these metabolites are conjugated at various levels.

Phytochemical metabolites are chemically distinct from their parent compounds, differing in size, polarity, and ionic form. Consequently, their physiological behavior is likely to differ from that of the parent compounds. This distinction is crucial when interpreting the potential clinical significance of in vitro studies using grape phytochemicals. Conversely, studies involving whole grapes, which are physiologically relevant, are essential for understanding the link between grape product intake and disease. For grapes to have therapeutic effects in tissues, the bioavailability of one or more grape-derived phytochemicals in the tissue is likely required. An effect of grape phytochemicals on transcription factors or cell signaling would likely require their availability in tissues, and their distribution among tissues is uneven. For example, in similar long-term feeding studies, anthocyanins have demonstrated bioavailability in several tissues. As expected, phytochemical levels are highest in organs responsible for metabolism and elimination. Importantly, these animals are typically fasted before sacrifice and show no detectable plasma anthocyanins while showing diverse tissue deposition. We demonstrated that some anthocyanins from tart cherries are present in the brain and heart after just three weeks of feeding a one percent tart cherry diet. However, their concentrations were found to be two to five times higher in the bladder, kidneys, and liver. As reported, the food matrix, along with the organism's behavior and response factors, may play a significant role in bioavailability. Furthermore, the specific serum kinetic behavior could impact the bioactivity of phenolic compounds. These authors observed the possible influence of anthocyanins on leptin levels and the associated pathways in chronic metabolic diseases. It was revealed that anthocyanins have the potential to modulate adipose tissue hypertrophy and leptin expression by interacting with T N F-alpha, C C A A T enhancer-binding protein, peroxisome proliferator-activated receptor, C A M P response element-binding protein, and sterol regulatory element-binding protein-1. Moreover, these bioactive compounds modulate leptin receptors and promote the phosphorylation of Janus kinase 2, signal transducer and activator of transcription 3, phosphoinositide 3-kinase, and protein kinase B pathways. They concluded that flavonoid anthocyanins help overcome metabolic disorder outcomes, such as lipid accumulation, insulin resistance, and gut microbiota imbalance. However, direct evidence of these and other flavonoids in signal transduction needs to be proven at the molecular binding and gene regulation levels.

Bioavailable grape phytochemicals, their enterohepatic metabolites, and their intracellular metabolites may interact with sulfhydryl moieties on kinase proteins and alter secondary protein structure and activity. The exact kinase signaling pathways involved in the observed grape-related effects are unknown and require further investigation in various tissues. Research indicates that grape phytochemical metabolites can indeed modulate various cell signaling pathways. These include the phosphoinositide 3-kinase, Akt and protein kinase B, tyrosine kinases, protein kinase C, and mitogen-activated protein kinases. These pathways are important in regulating inflammation and metabolic processes, making grape phytochemicals potentially beneficial in managing long-term inflammation and metabolic syndrome.

In the context of inflammation-related gene transcription, the effects of bioavailable grape phytochemicals on N F-kappa B activity may be mediated indirectly, rather than through direct interaction. However, these phytochemicals can exert direct influences on upstream kinase signaling pathways and subsequent gene transcription and translation processes. In addition to our results in hypertensive rats, we previously showed in obese rats with metabolic syndrome that grape intake reduced N F-kappa B activity and related messenger ribonucleic acid and protein. As expected, the effects of grapes were most pronounced in the kidney and liver but were also present in the heart, brain, and skeletal muscle. These results could support many interesting hypotheses for further study in models of metabolic syndrome and type 2 diabetes.

Possible Action of Grapes to Prevent the Development of Metabolic Syndrome. Grapes can play a significant role in preventing metabolic syndrome due to their rich content of bioactive compounds. As discussed above, these phytochemicals have several modes of action, including the activation and deactivation of various transcription factors, the upregulation and downregulation of key genes, and the inhibition or activation of several key enzymes involved in the development of metabolic syndrome.

Grapes have been shown to reduce blood pressure, a key factor in metabolic syndrome. They help lower triglycerides and low-density lipoprotein cholesterol while increasing high-density lipoprotein cholesterol. The various bioactive compounds in grapes can reduce inflammation, which is often associated with metabolic syndrome. Additionally, grapes promote the growth of healthy gut bacteria, such as Akkermansia muciniphila, which is beneficial for metabolic health.

The regular consumption of grapes can also improve glucose tolerance, helping manage blood sugar levels. Incorporating grapes into a balanced diet, along with other healthy lifestyle choices, can be a proactive step toward preventing metabolic syndrome.

Glycemic Control with Nutrients and Bioactive Compounds in Grapes. Grapes can play a beneficial role in managing hyperglycemia, a key factor in metabolic syndrome, which can develop from high blood sugar levels. Despite containing natural sugars, grapes have several bioactive compounds that positively impact blood sugar regulation and improve glycemic levels. Resveratrol and various flavonoids, well-known for their anti-inflammatory and antioxidant properties, work synergistically to address complications associated with type 2 diabetes.

Although grapes have a low to medium glycemic index of around 56, they release sugars into the bloodstream more gradually compared to other fruits, making them a better option for individuals with metabolic syndrome and type 2 diabetes. This slower release results in a moderate increase in blood sugar levels, unlike fruits with a high glycemic index. For instance, watermelon has a glycemic index of approximately 72, leading to a rapid spike in blood glucose levels. While the glycemic index is a valuable tool for selecting healthy fruits, the glycemic load is another important measure to consider for metabolic syndrome. Interestingly, grapes have a low glycemic load of about 9.6, which is significant for recommending them to at-risk populations. A low glycemic load indicates that the carbohydrate content in a typical serving size provides a more accurate picture of how a food impacts blood sugar levels. For example, a serving of grapes has a lower glycemic load compared to a serving of bananas, which have a glycemic load of around 12.

The potential benefits of low-glycemic-index fruits lie in their high fiber content relative to their sugar levels. However, it is noteworthy that the benefits of grapes for blood sugar management are more related to their overall nutrient profile, including antioxidants and vitamins, rather than a high fiber-to-sugar ratio.

To prevent the development of metabolic syndrome, type 2 diabetes, and their complications, it is crucial to manage blood sugar levels and minimize spikes in blood glucose. This can be achieved through various methods, including regular blood glucose monitoring, counting carbohydrates, and considering the glycemic index and glycemic load for dietary planning.

The polyphenols found in grapes, particularly resveratrol, play a central role in reducing blood sugar levels and improving insulin sensitivity. Several studies have shown that resveratrol may enhance insulin function. Improved insulin sensitivity from grape products, as observed in rodent studies, has been reported to help manage hyperglycemia. Additionally, several minerals present in grapes, particularly potassium, play a vital role in maintaining normal blood pressure and reducing the risk of hypertension and cardiovascular diseases. It is well known that potassium can balance sodium levels in the body, promoting cardiac health and potentially reducing the risk of stroke.

Grapes can be a valuable addition to a diet aimed at managing hyperglycemia. Their low glycemic load, wide range of bioactive compounds, and beneficial nutrients make them a heart-healthy and diabetes-friendly fruit. Consuming grapes alongside other nutrient-rich foods can support healthy living and help control metabolic health, potentially preventing complications related to metabolic syndrome.

Insulin resistance is a key factor in metabolic dysfunction and hyperglycemia. It is characterized by the body's reduced biological response to circulating insulin and is a target for bioactive compounds with the potential to improve these conditions. Insulin resistance is also linked to various pathological conditions, including nonalcoholic fatty liver disease, endothelial dysfunction, and chronic kidney disease. In the case of obesity, an imbalance in the production of pro- and anti-inflammatory adipocytokines can lead to metabolic complications and insulin resistance. These conditions can be prevented or managed with a well-balanced diet, in which grapes may be particularly beneficial due to their bioactive compounds. Recent studies in animal and human models have shown promising results for insulin-sensitizing supplements derived from grapes, including grape-derived flavonoids and resveratrol. Although the pharmacological properties and clinical applications of these products are still being explored, their metabolic benefits appear to be compound-specific and may involve gene expression regulation through the nuclear high-mobility group A1 protein.

Action of Grape Bioactive Compounds on Hypercholesterolemia. Hypercholesterolemia, characterized by elevated cholesterol levels in the blood, is a significant risk factor for the development of metabolic syndrome and type 2 diabetes. The effective management of cholesterol levels is essential for reducing the risk of myocardial infarction and cerebrovascular accidents, as well as for maintaining overall cardiovascular health. Various pharmacological treatments and preventive medications are available to lower cholesterol levels. However, a natural and holistic approach is often advocated, utilizing naturally occurring cholesterol-lowering herbs and fruits. Grapes, in particular, are recognized as a natural source of cholesterol regulation due to their phytochemicals, which have been demonstrated to modulate cholesterol levels through their bioactive properties. These compounds are potent antioxidants and anti-inflammatory agents. They also inhibit several key regulatory enzymes, helping reduce oxidative stress, a major factor in the development of atherosclerosis and other cardiovascular diseases. One important aspect of these compounds is their multitarget activity. The cholesterol biosynthesis pathway is well studied, with several rate-limiting steps and biosynthetic enzymes characterized. One of the important enzymes involved in cholesterol biosynthesis is 3-hydroxy-3-methylglutaryl-coenzyme A reductase.

This enzyme is critical in the biosynthesis of cholesterol. Inhibitors of H M G-C o A reductase, known as statins, are widely used to lower low-density lipoprotein levels and reduce the risk of cardiovascular diseases. Additionally, by scavenging free radicals and preventing lipid peroxidation, grape polyphenolics prevent the oxidation of L D L cholesterol, a critical step in plaque formation within arteries.

The whole grape or its bioactive compounds also possess anti-inflammatory effects. Chronic inflammation is closely linked to hypercholesterolemia and is prone to developing cardiovascular diseases. The relationship between chronic inflammation and hypercholesterolemia is well established and documented by several studies. Grape polyphenols inhibit the biosynthesis of inflammatory cytokines and enzymes, thereby reducing inflammation in blood vessels.

Grape polyphenolics improve endothelial function, which is crucial for vascular health. Several polyphenolics, including resveratrol, enhance the production of nitric oxide. They upregulate the expression of endothelial nitric oxide synthase, stimulate endothelial nitric oxide synthase enzymatic activity, and prevent endothelial nitric oxide synthase uncoupling. This can improve vascular function and promote vasodilation, benefiting the cardiovascular system. It also reduces blood pressure and improves conditions related to hypertension and its complications. Additionally, grape polyphenolics have shown inhibitory effects against angiotensin-converting enzyme. Angiotensin-converting enzyme works within the renin-angiotensin system by transforming angiotensin one into angiotensin two. The latter is considered a powerful vasoconstrictor. Another important function of this enzyme is related to the deactivation of the vasodilator bradykinin. Therefore, the inhibition of angiotensin-converting enzyme can be beneficial in regulating hypertension by affecting bradykinin metabolism. Our research with several polyphenolics supports this statement and highlights the potential of grape polyphenolics in modulating these pathways, contributing to their metabolic health benefits.

Grape Polyphenolics and Their Impact on Chronic Inflammation. Inflammation is a natural immune response to injury or infection. However, chronic inflammation can contribute to the development of diseases, such as cardiovascular conditions, diabetes, and cancer. Grape polyphenolics, including flavonoids, phenolic acids, and resveratrol, have been extensively studied for their potent anti-inflammatory properties. These compounds can inhibit the production of pro-inflammatory cytokines, such as tumor necrosis factor-alpha, interleukin-1 beta, and interleukin-6, which are central to the inflammatory response. Their overproduction is linked to chronic inflammation and related complications. By reducing these cytokines, grape polyphenolics can help mitigate inflammation. The regulation of transcription factors is also crucial in this process. The N F-kappa B pathway, a key regulator of inflammation, leads to the expression of various inflammatory genes. Grape polyphenolics, particularly resveratrol, inhibit the activation of N F-kappa B, reducing the expression of inflammatory mediators and enzymes, such as cyclooxygenase-2 and inducible nitric oxide synthase.

Oxidative stress is closely linked to inflammation. Some grape polyphenolics possess strong antioxidant properties that neutralize reactive oxygen species and reduce oxidative stress. By lowering oxidative stress, these compounds indirectly reduce inflammation as oxidative stress can activate inflammatory pathways. Certain enzymes, such as cyclooxygenase and lipoxygenase, play a role in the inflammatory process by producing pro-inflammatory mediators like prostaglandins and leukotrienes. Grape polyphenolics can inhibit these enzymes, thereby reducing the production of these inflammatory mediators. Additionally, grape polyphenolics can enhance the production of anti-inflammatory cytokines, such as interleukin-10, to help balance the immune response and promote the resolution of inflammation. Inflammation plays a substantial role in the development and progression of metabolic syndrome.

Understanding these connections highlights the importance of managing inflammation to prevent or mitigate the development of metabolic syndrome. Lifestyle changes, such as a healthy diet and regular exercise, can help reduce inflammation and also improve metabolic health.

Grape and Obesity, Metabolic Dysregulation, and Type 2 Diabetes. Grapes may significantly influence the development of obesity and type 2 diabetes mellitus. Recent studies confirm their effectiveness against obesity and its various components, primarily due to their impact on metabolic dysregulation, a major factor in the development of these conditions. Several studies indicate that grape polyphenolics impact lipid metabolism, potentially affecting fatty acid synthesis, fat deposition, and triglyceride breakdown, which may help prevent obesity development. However, the mechanisms are not yet well elucidated. The presumed action mechanisms are diverse, including signaling pathways and direct effects on various enzyme activities. Numerous animal studies and their results confirm the pivotal role of these polyphenols in addressing pathologies related to fat storage and metabolism. These pathologies often lead to conditions such as obesity, insulin resistance, and dyslipidemia. Chuang with coworkers studied the impact of grape and grape extract on glucose tolerance and inflammation in obese mice. Mice were fed high-fat diets supplemented with three percent grape pomace or 0.02 percent grape extract for 18 weeks. Grape powder supplementation decreased markers of inflammation by approximately 20 to 50 percent in both serum and adipose tissue. A recent study investigated whether long-term supplementation of grapes could improve oxidative stress, inflammation, and hyperglycemia associated with Western diet-induced obesity. The study indicates that grapes protect against Western diet-induced obesity and hepatic steatosis by diminishing the portal delivery of lipogenic butyrate and sugars due to their increased intestinal utilization. These and other findings suggest that grape supplementation may serve as an effective dietary intervention for reducing these pathologies. Young diet-induced obese mice were randomly divided into three treatment groups: a standard diet group, a Western high-fat diet group, and a Western diet plus grape seed extract group at 2.4 grams grape seed extract per kilogram diet. By week 12, diet-induced obese mice in the grape seed extract group gained significantly more weight than the Western diet and standard groups; the high-fat diet groups gained 80 percent more weight than the standard diet group. Eight of 12 mice in the Western diet group, compared to only 1 of 12 mice in the grape seed extract group, had fasting blood glucose levels above 140 milligrams per deciliter. Mice in the grape seed extract group also had 21 percent lower fasting blood glucose and 17.1 percent lower C-reactive protein levels than mice in the Western diet group. However, grape seed extract supplementation did not affect oxidative stress in diet-induced obesity, as determined by plasma oxygen radical absorbance capacity, glutathione peroxidase, and liver lipid peroxidation.

In a study by Gourineni and coworkers, C57BL/6 J mice were given a low-fat diet at 10 percent kilocalories fat, high-fat diet at 60 percent kilocalories fat, high-fat plus 0.4 percent muscadine grape phytochemicals, or high-fat plus 0.4 percent muscadine wine phytochemicals for 15 weeks. At 7 weeks, mice fed high-fat plus muscadine grape phytochemicals had significantly decreased body weights by 12 percent compared to high-fat controls. Dietary muscadine grape phytochemicals or muscadine wine phytochemicals supplementation reduced the plasma content of free fatty acids, triglycerides, and cholesterol in obese mice. Serum inflammation was attenuated, while the activity of serum glutathione peroxidase was enhanced.

Hogan and colleagues characterized the effects of grape pomace extract in male diet-induced obese mice. Mice were randomly divided into three treatment groups: groups given a normal diet, a high-fat diet, and a high-fat diet supplemented with grape pomace extract. After 12-week treatment, mice in the high-fat diet groups gained 29 percent more weight than the normal diet group. The grape pomace extract supplementation, estimated at 250 milligrams per kilogram body weight per day, lowered plasma C-reactive protein levels by 15.5 percent in the high-fat-diet-fed mice, suggesting a potential anti-inflammatory effect of dietary grape pomace extract. However, dietary grape pomace extract did not improve oxidative stress in diet-induced obese mice, as determined by plasma oxygen radical absorbance capacity, glutathione peroxidase, and liver lipid peroxidation.

In a study by Mokrani and coworkers, treatments in obese rats, and to a lesser extent in nonobese rats, decreased the weight of various adipose tissues and the serum levels of cholesterol, L D L, triglycerides, lipase, and C-reactive protein while increasing H D L and adiponectin. Grapes exert beneficial effects in obese rats and partially restore the observed dysbiosis. Interestingly, the combination of grape extract with the weight-loss medication orlistat produced the most pronounced beneficial effect. Moreover, the various treatments could also enhance physiological and gut microbiota modifications in nonobese rats.

Kim and colleagues investigated whether resveratrol attenuates high-fat diet-induced adipogenesis and inflammation in the epididymal fat tissues of mice. In comparison with high-fat diet-fed mice, mice fed with a 0.4 percent resveratrol-supplemented diet showed significantly lower body weight gain at 48 percent, visceral fat pad weights at 58 percent, and plasma levels of triglyceride, free fatty acids, total cholesterol, glucose, T N F-alpha, and monocyte chemoattractant protein 1. Resveratrol significantly attenuated the high-fat diet-induced upregulation of pro-inflammatory cytokines, including T N F-alpha, interferon alpha, interferon beta, and I L-6, and their upstream signaling molecules, including Toll-like receptor 2 and 4, myeloid differentiation primary response, Toll-interleukin 1 receptor domain-containing adaptor protein, T I R-domain-containing adapter-inducing interferon-beta, T N F receptor-associated factor 6, interferon regulatory factor 5, phosphorylated interferon regulatory factor 3, and N F-kappa B, in the adipose tissue.

Resveratrol undergoes extensive metabolism, including conjugation, hydrolyzation, and enterohepatic circulation. Ex vivo studies on adipose tissue show that resveratrol inhibits adipogenesis and prevents triglyceride accumulation by affecting peroxisome proliferator-activated receptor gamma and sirtuin 1 expression. Animal studies support resveratrol's anti-inflammatory effects. Limited human studies indicate that resveratrol enhances insulin sensitivity and fasting glucose levels in patients with type 2 diabetes mellitus and may reduce inflammation in obesity. Evidence suggests that resveratrol's metabolic benefits are linked to interactions with gut microbiota and the modulation of protein targets, such as sirtuins and peroxisome proliferator-activated receptor gamma. However, despite promising results, their optimal dosage and general potential benefits are not yet determined.

Terra and colleagues evaluated the effect of procyanidin intake on inflammatory mediators in obesity-prone rats fed hyperlipidemic diet. Male Zucker fa/fa rats were randomly grouped to receive a low-fat diet, a high-fat diet, or a high-fat diet supplemented with procyanidins from grape seed at 3.45 milligrams per kilogram feed for 19 weeks. The procyanidin-enriched diet decreased rat plasma C-reactive protein levels but not I L-6 levels. The decrease in plasma C-reactive protein in procyanidin-treated rats was related to reduced C-reactive protein messenger ribonucleic acid expression in the liver and mesenteric white adipose tissue and decreased expression of the pro-inflammatory cytokines T N F-alpha and I L-6 in mesenteric white adipose tissue. In contrast, adiponectin messenger ribonucleic acid increased in these tissues. Plasma C-reactive protein levels correlated positively with C-reactive protein expression in the mesenteric white adipose tissue, suggesting that procyanidin extract modulates C-reactive protein transcription and translation. The expression of the anti-inflammatory cytokine adiponectin correlated negatively with T N F-alpha and I L-6 in the mesenteric white adipose tissue.

In a subsequent study, Terra and colleagues tested the hypothesis that grape seed procyanidin extract would improve local and systemic inflammation in diet-induced obesity. Rats were fed a 60 percent kilocalories fat diet with or without grape seed procyanidins at 30 milligrams per kilogram per day for 19 weeks. Then rats were provided a cafeteria diet for 13 weeks to investigate the corrective effects of two procyanidin extract doses, 25 and 50 milligrams per kilogram per day, for 10 and 30 days. In the preventive model fed a 60 percent fat diet, procyanidin extract decreased body weight; reduced plasma T N F-alpha and C-reactive protein; increased adiponectin expression; decreased T N F-alpha, interleukin-6, and C-reactive protein expression in mesenteric white adipose tissue; and reduced muscle T N F-alpha. Procyanidin extract also reduced liver N F-kappa B activity. Finally, procyanidin extract dietary supplementation was linked to reduced expression of Emr1, a marker of macrophage F4/80, which suggests reduced macrophage infiltration of white adipose tissue. In the corrective model, however, only the high dose of procyanidin extract reduced plasma C-reactive protein and had no effect on plasma T N F-alpha.

Barona and colleagues evaluated the effects of grape consumption on inflammation and oxidation in the presence or absence of dyslipidemias in metabolic syndrome. Men with metabolic syndrome, 11 with high triglycerides and low H D L and 13 with no dyslipidemia, were randomly assigned to consume daily either 46 grams of lyophilized grape powder, equivalent to 252 grams fresh grapes, or placebo with an identical macronutrient composition and caloric value as grape powder for 4 weeks. After a 3-week washout, participants followed the alternate treatment. Plasma adiponectin and interleukin-10 were increased in the grape powder compared to the placebo only in those individuals without dyslipidemia. Additionally, plasma interleukin-10 was negatively correlated with nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide phosphate oxidase 2 expression, a marker of oxidative stress, while inducible nitric oxide synthase expression was positively correlated with the expression of superoxide dismutase 2, a key antioxidant enzyme.

Kelishadi and colleagues determined the short- and long-term effects of the consumption of grape juice on inflammation in adolescents with metabolic syndrome. Fifteen individuals drank natural grape juice for 1 month. Measurements of inflammatory factors, including high-sensitivity C-reactive protein, soluble E-selectin, soluble intercellular adhesion molecule-1, soluble vascular cell adhesion molecule, and I L-6, and flow-mediated dilation were made at baseline, 4 hours after first juice consumption, and after 1 month of juice consumption. The percent changes of flow-mediated dilation were significant in both the short and long term. Soluble E-selectin and I L-6 were significantly decreased after 1 month. A significant inverse correlation was observed between changes in I L-6 levels and flow-mediated dilation after one month of supplementation.

In the obesity-prone Zucker fatty rats, we tested the effect of an American-style diet supplemented with whole-table grape powder at 3 percent weight per weight. The rats were fed for 90 days. Compared to the macronutrient and calorie-matched control group, grape intake significantly reduced serum C-reactive protein, T N F-alpha, and I L-6. Grape also reduced liver, kidney, and abdominal fat weight among several other phenotypes. The pharmacodynamic effect was measured by changes in N F-kappa B-related and antioxidant-related messenger ribonucleic acid and proteins in the heart, abdominal fat, skeletal muscle, liver, brain, renal cortex, and renal medulla. N F-kappa B-related messenger ribonucleic acid and proteins appear most significantly reduced in liver and abdominal fat. Antioxidant defense messenger ribonucleic acid and proteins were most significantly increased in the liver and renal cortex.

Leonetti and colleagues investigated the effects of procyanidin-enriched grape seed extract on obesity-related cardiovascular and metabolic disorders, focusing on the role of the estrogen receptor alpha. They found that grape seed extract reduced adiposity, plasma triglycerides, and oxidative stress in various tissues of mice fed a Western diet but did not improve vascular dysfunction. The beneficial effects of the extract were diminished in mice lacking estrogen receptor alpha, indicating that estrogen receptor alpha plays a significant role in mediating these effects. Overall, this study suggests that grape seed extract could be a promising dietary supplement for mitigating metabolic syndrome through estrogen receptor alpha-dependent mechanisms.

The review by Santa and colleagues highlights the importance of grape phytochemicals in maintaining gut health and preventing metabolic syndrome. It emphasizes that a combination of these phytochemicals with vitamin D can improve gut microbiota and immunity, reducing the risk of obesity, diabetes, and related diseases. The review also notes that nurturing good gut microbiota with prebiotics and probiotics can suppress inflammation and enhance protective adipokines, contributing to overall health.

Grapes Impact on Cardiovascular Disease. More specific research has been conducted with resveratrol, a well-known compound found in grapes. Resveratrol is recognized for its potent antioxidant properties and also offers anti-inflammatory and heart-protective benefits. However, its development as a drug faces challenges, such as low solubility, poor bioavailability, and possible side effects. There are several clinical trials conducted so far, and proposed strategies are to make resveratrol a viable therapeutic option. Resveratrol has demonstrated significant protective effects in heart cells under ischemia-reperfusion conditions. Studies have found that resveratrol significantly improves heart function in rats and prevents an increase in autophagy caused by ischemia-reperfusion. It may protect against heart injury by modulating the D J-1 and mitogen-activated protein kinase kinase kinase 1 and c-Jun N-terminal Kinase pathways, suggesting a novel approach for maintaining heart health.

Tome-Carneiro and colleagues investigated the effects of resveratrol-rich grape supplements on the inflammatory and fibrinolytic status of subjects at high risk of cardiovascular disease and treated with statins. Seventy-five patients consumed either a placebo, a resveratrol-rich grape supplement containing 8 milligrams resveratrol, or a conventional grape supplement lacking resveratrol for the first 6 months and a double dose for the next 6 months. In contrast to placebo and conventional grape supplements, the resveratrol-rich grape supplement significantly decreased high sensitivity C-reactive protein, T N F-alpha, plasminogen activator inhibitor type 1, and I L-6 to I L-10 ratio but increased anti-inflammatory I L-10.

Tome-Carneiro and colleagues examined the effect of resveratrol-containing grape extracts on the transcriptional profiles of inflammatory genes in peripheral blood mononuclear cells in patients with stable coronary artery disease. After 1 year of supplementation, in contrast to the placebo and conventional grape extract groups, the resveratrol-containing grape extract group showed an increase in the anti-inflammatory serum adiponectin. In addition, six key inflammation-related transcription factors were predicted to be significantly activated or inhibited. Twenty-seven extracellular-space-acting genes involved in inflammation, cell migration, and T-cell interaction signals were downregulated by grape extract. These authors also investigated the molecular changes in peripheral blood mononuclear cells associated with a 1-year intake of a resveratrol-enriched grape extract in hypertensive male patients with type 2 diabetes. Supplementation reduced serum I L-6. The peripheral blood mononuclear cell expression of the pro-inflammatory cytokines C-C motif chemokine ligand 3, I L-1 beta, and T N F-alpha was significantly reduced, and the expression of the transcriptional repressor leucine-rich repeat flightless-interacting protein-1 was increased in peripheral blood mononuclear cells. Also, many micro R N As involved in the regulation of inflammation, including miR-21, miR-181b, miR-663, miR-30c2, miR-155, and miR-34a, were highly correlated and altered by grape extract.

Clinical Evidence. Several clinical studies have supported the beneficial effects of grape polyphenolics on cholesterol levels. For instance, a systematic review and meta-analysis published in the British Journal of Nutrition found that grape seed extract supplementation decreased serum levels of L D L cholesterol and triglycerides and supports the beneficial effects of grape polyphenolics on cholesterol levels. Although the study did not find a significant overall effect on H D L cholesterol, it did report improvements in studies with shorter intervention periods and lower dosages of grape seed extract. Another study highlighted the role of resveratrol, a specific polyphenolic found in grape skins, in reducing cholesterol levels and improving heart health. Incorporating grape polyphenolics into the diet can be achieved through the consumption of grapes, grape juice, and moderate amounts of red wine. Additionally, grape seed extract supplements are available for those seeking concentrated doses of these beneficial compounds. Grape polyphenolics offer a natural and effective approach to managing hypercholesterolemia and improving cholesterol levels. Their antioxidant and anti-inflammatory properties, along with their ability to prevent lipid damage, make them a valuable addition to a heart-healthy lifestyle. Clinical studies have shown that grape polyphenolics have been found to reduce oxidative stress and inflammation, which are key factors in the development of metabolic syndrome.

By incorporating grape polyphenolics into the diet, individuals can take proactive steps toward better metabolic health and a reduced risk of type 2 diabetes or heart disease.

Conclusion and Future Directions. Grapes are not only a delicious and versatile fruit but also a rich source of bioactive compounds that can play a crucial role in preventing and managing several health conditions, including metabolic syndrome, as presented in this chapter. Numerous studies highlight several possible targets and mechanisms of action, focusing on the polyphenolic and other bioactive compounds derived from grapes. These compounds have demonstrated significant antioxidant and anti-inflammatory activities, as well as other potentials that can prevent the development of metabolic syndrome and its complications.

By continuing to explore and understand these phytochemicals and their mechanisms of action, we can unlock new opportunities for improving human health and well-being. Ongoing research paves the way for innovative dietary recommendations and therapeutic strategies, highlighting the potential of grapes in the prevention and management of metabolic syndrome and other health conditions.

The bioavailability of these compounds is a key factor in their effectiveness. Challenges are still present to enhance the bioavailability of many grape phytochemicals. Continued research and clinical studies are essential to fully understand the synergistic effects of these compounds and to develop effective dietary recommendations and therapeutic strategies.

Pharmacovigilance will be crucial in monitoring the safety and efficacy of grape-derived products, ensuring that any adverse effects are promptly identified and managed.

To effectively integrate grapes into our diet and offer precise recommendations to nutritionists, public health professionals, and physicians, the following key and emerging aspects should be considered for future research: first, bioavailability and bioaccessibility: the effectiveness of grape-derived natural products relies on how well they are absorbed and utilized within the body; second, pleiotropic effects of phytochemicals: several constituents can act on various subcellular compartments, enhancing their therapeutic potential; and third, potential synergistic interactions: the combined effects of various bioactive compounds derived from grapes can enhance their overall efficacy.

The future directions of grape research and their translational benefits encompass several key areas. One crucial aspect is sustainable agriculture, which involves supporting grape growers and promoting sustainable practices to ensure a steady supply of high-quality grapes. Another important direction is the development of new products, leveraging the bioactive compounds in grapes to create innovative dietary supplements, functional foods, and beverages. Public education also plays a vital role. Educating consumers about the health benefits of grapes and encouraging their inclusion in daily diets are essential. Additionally, robust pharmacovigilance programs are necessary to continuously monitor and evaluate the safety of grape-derived products in various practical applications. These programs ensure that any potential risks are identified and managed effectively, maintaining consumer trust and safety.
