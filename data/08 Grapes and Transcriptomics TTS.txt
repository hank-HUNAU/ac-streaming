Grapes and Transcriptomics: Fueling the Nutrigenomics Era. Eun-Jung Park, Asim Dave, and John M. Pezzuto.

Abstract. Millions of tons of fresh table grapes are consumed by the general population each year, primarily as a snack, raising the question of whether there are any health benefits. Preclinical and clinical trials have indicated there are health benefits, either directly or indirectly, via prolongation of healthspan. The array of responses with seemingly independent conditions such as cardiovascular, neurological, and metabolic disorders is astonishing but suggests a commonality leading to amelioration. Issues such as oxidative stress and inflammation are often cited as generic causative problems, and modification of disease-specific targets and pathways is often investigated. Functioning through these particular mechanisms is certainly possible given the scores of phytochemicals found in grapes. However, a unifying mode of action seems more satisfying: a broad-based nutrigenomic response. In general, systemic responses may be actuated by modulation of the microbiome or metabolome, for example, but here we focus on the genome. Grapes have been shown in both animal models and humans to significantly modify gene expression in ways that can be understood as being consistent with disease prevention. These expression patterns vary from organ to organ, from person to person, and between males and females, yet in every case, the expression patterns differ before and after grape consumption. This suggests a commonality: a resetting of homeostasis. We know that consumption of grapes is not harmful, we know that consumption leads to measurable phenotypic changes, and we know that clinical trials have indicated promising potential for ameliorating human ailments. It seems likely that such molecular and physiological alterations by grapes have consequences relevant to disease prevention. Although the gene expression changes as a downstream response resulting from transcriptional regulation are extraordinarily intricate and complicated, they are not insurmountable to study. With the available technology, we can unravel these processes. By understanding them, perhaps using grapes as a whole food prototype, we may gain a clearer understanding of how to improve and extend healthspan.

Diet and Health, Longevity, and Health Span. Many studies on food and health focus on strategies for extending lifespan, including caloric restriction, low-fat diets, veganism, and the Mediterranean diet. Historically, the pursuit of longevity, reminiscent of Emperor Qin Shi Huang's quest for the elixir of life, has evolved into a modern emphasis on health span: the period of life spent in good health. It may be speculated that some molecular interventions, involving select targets such as sirtuins or telomeres, advancements in omics technologies, including genomics, transcriptomics, and proteomics, gene therapies, or application of A I-driven platforms, may be brought to bear for the extension of lifespan. Time will tell, but presently, dietary modifications remain a practical and effective approach for promoting long-term health.

As food is a daily and fundamental component of human biology, its persistent but moderate influence plays a crucial role in health maintenance. The Mediterranean and Okinawan diets are widely touted to be effective in this capacity. The longevity of people living in so-called Blue Zones, places where people reach age 100 at 10 times greater rates than in the United States, has been attributed to nine commonalities, called the Power 9, several of which relate to diet. Other success stories include the Finnish Geriatric Intervention Study to Prevent Cognitive Impairment and Disability study and the Prospective Urban Rural Epidemiology study. The F I N G E R study demonstrated that a multidomain intervention could improve or maintain cognitive functioning in at-risk elderly people. The P U R E study reports that high carbohydrate intake is associated with a higher risk of total mortality, whereas total fat and individual types of fat were related to lower total mortality. Saturated fat had an inverse association with stroke. Investigating these effects requires a comprehensive approach that accounts for the complex interactions between dietary compounds, the gut microbiome, and systemic metabolism. An additional confounding factor is individual variability. Even in controlled animal studies using inbred mice, prolonged dietary interventions result in significant inter-individual variability in gene expression, making it difficult to avoid large standard deviations. In humans, this variability is even more pronounced due to the additional influence of the exposome, encompassing environmental factors, diet, lifestyle, and stress.

Despite these complexities and diverse genetic backgrounds, it seems intuitive that sustained dietary habits significantly contribute to longevity and, conversely, to many diseases, such as metabolic disorders to neurodegenerative conditions. While diet may exert only subtle daily effects, these cumulative influences over time can profoundly shape health outcomes.

Assessing the direct impact of specific foods on health remains challenging. Irrespective of the food of interest, certain confounders equally apply. First, a single dietary ingredient can vary in bioactive composition based on its cultivation region, agricultural practices, and environmental conditions. Additionally, the way food is prepared and cooked alters its bioavailability and nutrient composition, adding another layer of complexity. Also, since food is ingested orally, dietary components are subject to modification by gut microbiota, as well as first-pass metabolism in the liver. Consequently, consuming a single food item may lead to many metabolites entering the body, rather than just those associated with the unprocessed food, and these metabolites are likely to contribute to additional physiological effects.

Human responses to these variations further complicate standardization. Consequently, when analyzing transcriptomic data related to food, research often focuses more on horticultural and agricultural aspects, such as genome-wide expression changes associated with grape variety, skin pigmentation, or cultivation history, rather than the direct impact of food on human gene expression. Moreover, the way food is prepared and cooked alters its bioavailability and nutrient composition, adding another layer of complexity.

In spite of these challenges, grapes have been evaluated for their potential health benefits, including many human clinical trials. A key factor that has enabled the conduct of these studies is the provision of a standardized product, prepared in a manner suitable for human consumption, legitimately representative of the grape as a whole-food, and made available on an ongoing basis.

Grapes as a Dietary Component. Grapes have profoundly influenced human civilization, impacting agriculture and culture over thousands of years. Traces of grape seeds, stalks, and skins have been found in Neolithic vessels from sites in China and the Middle East, roughly 6000 to 10,000 years ago. A recent genetic analysis of cultivated and wild grape varieties reveals that domestication occurred concurrently in multiple regions including Western Asia and the Caucasus. The domesticated grapevine, Vitis vinifera subspecies vinifera, has been integral not only as a food source but also as the foundation of winemaking. Early farmers selected grapevines for traits such as sweetness and resilience. Then, human migration facilitated cultural exchanges that led to widespread cultivation and the development of diverse varieties, especially in regions like Italy. Over time, selective breeding further enhanced grapevines' flavor, aroma, and adaptability.

Like some other fruits, grapes are a unique commodity. Many components of the diet are not individually consumed. For example, onions are rarely consumed in isolation; they are typically combined with other ingredients in prepared dishes. This makes it difficult to assess their standalone biological effects. On the other hand, rather than one of the dishes in a meal, or as a combined ingredient, people generally eat grapes as a snack. One serving is defined as three-quarters of a cup. The magnitude of consumption varies year by year, but in the modern world, human beings consume around 30 million tons of fresh table grapes each year. Considering this backdrop, it is reasonable to explore the potential effect of grapes on human health as an individual entity.

Indeed, as exemplified by the passages in this book, the physiological activity of grapes has been studied in a large number of preclinical and clinical investigations, in many cases, with promising results. In numerous studies, researchers have extracted bioactive compounds from food sources and investigated their mechanisms of action in isolation. As exemplified by one key component of grapes, resveratrol, this approach has value but tends to have little relationship with the real-life situation of consuming a whole food, fruit, or vegetable. Work with individual isolates is more geared toward the development of a drug or a dietary supplement. In terms of dietary influence, especially when a component of the diet is consumed in isolation, that is, as a snack, the question of great importance is how the supplemental dietary substance may function as a whole. This entails understanding the synergistic effects of multiple food-derived compounds. In the case of the grape, this corresponds to hundreds of different phytochemical components.

Targeted Actions Mediated by Grapes. Supposedly, the word superfood entered the American lexicon in the early twentieth century. It has since been applied to hundreds of foods, generally perceived as a nutrient-rich food that is considered to be beneficial for health and well-being. The term is not recognized by the F D A, and the headline of the American Heart Association dealing with this topic states "So-called superfoods alone won't make you healthier, but adding these nutritious foods to an already balanced diet can bring health benefits."

Logically, grapes and other berries are included in the list of foods grouped as superfoods. Such a classification is justified based on the results of many clinical and preclinical studies. The scope of potential health-promoting activities mediated by grapes, for example, metabolic syndrome, heart, eye, skin, etc., is noteworthy. This leads to the notion of a common mechanism leading to maladies, which often fall into the general categories of oxidative damage and inflammation. In turn, the antioxidant potential of superfoods, including grapes, is often emphasized.

In a simplistic way, the antioxidant potential of a dietary substance may be considered as direct interactions leading to the reduction of oxidative stress through quenching or scavenging. While this may play some role in the process, it is difficult to imagine achieving sufficiently high concentrations of the requisite antioxidants at the target site to facilitate efficient reactions. This leads to the idea of plant antioxidants functioning in an indirect manner based on gene expression. Consider, for example, the induction of the messenger R N A coding for an enzyme such as catalase, which catalyzes the reaction 2 hydrogen peroxide to oxygen plus water. The messenger R N A copy may facilitate the generation of 1000 copies of the enzyme, and an enzyme such as catalase has a turnover number of up to 4 times 10 to the 7 per second. In other words, the antioxidant potential may be enhanced by over 10 to the 10-fold per second.

Grapes regulate genes associated with inflammation, oxidative stress, and metabolism. Long-term grape consumption in animal models has been shown to enhance antioxidant defenses, mitigate chronic inflammatory responses, and influence epigenetic markers such as D N A methylation and histone modifications, potentially contributing to healthier aging. Several examples of specific targeted genes, the expression of which is influenced by grapes, are presented in the following section.

Some interactions with medications that result from the consumption of fruits and vegetables are also well-known, as exemplified by grapefruit. More recently, the broader impact of diet on mammalian homeostasis has been recognized. Thus, it is clear that grapes are broadly capable of modulating gene expression, as exemplified by numerous studies that have investigated targeted products relevant to the disease state being scrutinized. In many of the cases presented above, several other specific genes were interrogated with no effect on expression being observed. All of these data help us to understand the relevant mechanisms of grape action. Naturally, however, targeted studies are designed to investigate, reveal, and quantify the expression of only a limited set of entities. However, bearing in mind the concept of nutrigenomics, it is reasonable to expect that the expression of these particular genes only represents the tip of the iceberg. As described below, this is indeed the case, as demonstrated with various animal models and a limited number of human trials.

Nutrigenomics. The term nutrigenomics was first introduced publicly around the turn of the current century: "Nutrigenomics is the understanding of the effects of nutrients in molecular level processes in the body as well as the variable effects of nutrients and nonnutritive dietary phytochemicals have on each individual person." Over the years, along with the science, in addition to the broad term of nutrigenomics, how nutrients affect gene expression and molecular mechanisms, the lexicon is expanding to include terms such as nutriomes, nutrient combinations; nutrigenetics, how genetic variations influence individual responses to nutrients; nutriepigenetic, nutrient effects through epigenetic modifications; and the generic term nutritional genomics has come into play. The interplay and application of nutritional genomics to human disease states is widespread, for example, cardiovascular disease, metabolic disorders, cancer, circulatory system diseases, and obesity.

As illustrated in this monograph and elsewhere, grapes have been investigated for potential to ameliorate a wide range of disease states. From a superficial perspective, the ability of one dietary substance to modulate such a diversity array of maladies seems unfathomable. Nonetheless, the results of the studies are reliable, and taking the broad-based omics responses mediated by grape consumption into account, a rational explanation is beginning to emerge.

Nutrigenomics Effects of Fruits and Vegetables. On a fluctuating basis, dietary variations may lead to nutrigenomic responses. For example, dietary sugars, fats, and amino acids may control gene expression through carbohydrate-responsive element binding protein, peroxisome proliferator-activated receptors, and G C N 2 and activating transcription factor 4 and m T O R C 1 pathways, respectively. Homeostasis may be maintained by such systems, but sustained alterations may lead to conditions such as obesity, insulin resistance, type 2 diabetes, cardiovascular diseases, or cancer.

As described below, diet as a whole has a significant influence on the genotypic expression of human beings. In essence, changing the diet resets the homeostatic positioning of the individual. In the case of the Mediterranean or Okinawan-based Nordic diet, this is generally perceived as for the better. In addition to the impact of diet, a very long list of dietary food components, for example, vitamins such as vitamin D, vitamin A, vitamin E; polyphenols such as resveratrol, quercetin, catechins, curcumin; fatty acids such as omega-3 and omega-6; and minerals such as selenium and zinc, have been examined for potential to mediate various nutritional genomic responses, for example, gene expression, D N A methylation, histone modification, non-coding R N As, activation of transcription factors, ligand binding to the nuclear receptors.

We have adopted an intermediate approach between evaluating the effect of diet and evaluating the effect of individual dietary chemicals. It is fully appreciated that homeostasis may be affected by numerous factors such as exercise, sleep, stress, toxins, and pathogens in addition to diet. But nonetheless, diet is a dominant force, and the overall effect culminates from the overall diet. In our work with grapes, the focus has been on the whole food, not individual components such as resveratrol. While it is necessary to use a standardized product to assure scientific rigor, the standardized product is fully representative of grapes as a whole food, and we have taken care to administer quantities that are fully representative of quantities that are readily consumed by humans. In sum, our work is aimed at exploring how the addition of grapes to the normal diet of a free-living human being may lead to nutrigenomic changes that may be interpreted in relation to a person's health or risk of developing diseases.

Transcriptomic Effects Mediated by Grapes with Animal Models. While in vitro models are of value for studying dietary metabolites under controlled conditions, comprehensive in vivo investigations are necessary for understanding systemic interactions. For untargeted nutrigenomic studies, mice serve as a suitable model due, in part, to their extensive genomic characterization. As described below, we have investigated the potential of oral grape consumption to alter phenotypic expression patterns with mouse models, under a variety of experimental conditions. We have investigated a variety of tissues with males and females. We have taken care to use carefully controlled diets such that phenotypic changes can be attributed to the phytochemical constituents of grapes. Additionally, the quantity of grapes consumed by the mice was managed to correlate with a human consumption rate of one to two servings per day.

Influence of Grape Administration Concomitantly with a High-Fat Western-Style Diet. The effect of grapes was investigated in mice provided with a high-fat Western diet. Compared to the standard diet group, the group provided with 5 percent grape-supplemented diet showed enrichment in genes related to R N A processing, mitochondrial function, protein translation, drug metabolism, and oxidative stress pathways. Gene expression analysis revealed the greatest differences between the groups provided with the high-fat diet supplemented with 5 percent grape powder versus the high-fat diet control group. In the high-fat diet plus 5 percent grape powder group, 14 genes linked to fatty acid metabolism, transport, and sequestration were up-regulated, while genes associated with lipid accumulation and cholesterol synthesis, such as Plin4, Acaa1b, and Slc27a1, were down-regulated. Histological analysis indicated that a high-fat diet led to the development of nonalcoholic steatohepatitis, characterized by fatty liver markers, but grape supplementation mitigated these effects. Notably, survival time was improved in the high-fat diet plus 5 percent grape powder group compared to the high-fat diet group. These findings suggest that grapes may regulate hepatic gene expression, reduce oxidative damage, promote fatty acid metabolism, and alleviate nonalcoholic fatty liver disease, ultimately contributing to increased longevity in the context of a high-fat diet.

To further investigate metabolic changes, grape powder was incorporated into both standard and high-fat diets and fed to 10-week-old female C57BL/6 J mice for 91 days. At the end of the study, 24-hour urine samples were collected, and after a 12-hour fast, liver tissue was harvested for metabolomic analysis. Gas chromatography-mass spectrometry revealed alterations in urinary metabolites derived from gut microbiota. In the standard diet, grape supplementation reduced urinary levels of 4-hydroxyphenylacetic acid, 5-hydroxyindole, glyceric acid, gluconic acid, and myo-inositol. Conversely, in the high-fat diet, grape supplementation led to increased urinary levels of gluconic acid, scyllo-inositol, mannitol, xylitol, 5-hydroxyindole, and 2-deoxyribonic acid. Additionally, hepatic levels of ascorbic acid and 5-oxoproline were elevated, suggesting an antioxidant effect of grapes in the liver. Pathway enrichment analysis demonstrated that grape supplementation up-regulated the malate-aspartate shuttle in both standard and high-fat diet conditions, indicating enhanced hepatic glucose utilization for A T P production. These results highlight the potential of dietary grapes to modulate gut microbiota metabolism, mitigate hepatic oxidative stress, and improve energy efficiency in the liver.

Effect of Grapes on Brain. Supplementing a high-fat diet with 1 percent grape powder was found to reduce high-fat diet-induced anxiety-like behaviors. Genetic expression analysis in the brain revealed distinct differences among diet groups, with 210, 360, and 221 uniquely expressed genes in the standard diet, high-fat diet, and high-fat diet plus 1 percent grape groups, respectively. Cluster analysis showed that the high-fat diet plus 1 percent grape group's gene expression profile was more similar to the standard diet group than the high-fat diet group, suggesting a partial reversal of high-fat diet-induced changes. These findings indicate that dietary grape consumption can benefit behavior and cognition, counteracting the negative impact of a high-fat diet. The observed improvements are associated with widespread transcriptional changes in the brain, highlighting the potential of grapes in modulating neurobiological responses to dietary stressors.

Effect of Grapes on Skeletal Muscle. To investigate the effect of grapes on skeletal muscles, a mouse model was used to examine gene expression in males and females fed either a grape-enriched or a bland diet over 2.5 years. The results show sex-specific changes in gene expression within muscle tissue, with females exhibiting more pronounced alterations that trend toward a male-like phenotype. Up-regulation of genes such as Ahsg, Alb, Apoa1, and Arg1, together with down-regulation of Camp, Lcn2, and Irf4, suggested improved muscle function, a finding further supported by pathway analysis. A total of 25 differentially expressed genes associated with muscle health were identified, reinforcing the idea that long-term grape consumption may enhance muscle function. These findings suggest that grapes could have broader health benefits, particularly in modulating muscle physiology in a sex-dependent manner.

Effect of Grapes on Kidney. In the kidneys, the long-term addition of grapes to the diet showed protective effects, indicated by a reduction in B U N and creatinine ratios, which dropped from 125 to 63.7 in males and 152 to 40.4 in females. Histological results suggested that this improvement may be due to enhanced renal perfusion. Further, R N A sequencing analysis of the tissue revealed the up-regulation of 21 genes in males and 109 genes in females, with a corresponding downregulation of 108 and 65 genes, respectively. The negative shift in F P K M values of genes, such as alox5, btk, fga, fpr1, hmox1, lox, ltf, lyve1, marco, mmp8, prg4, s100a8 and 9, serpina3n, and vsig4 in males, and the positive shift of genes, such as camp, cd300lf, cd72, fcgr4, fgr, fpr2, htra4, il10, lilrb4b, lipg, pilra, and tlr8 in females, suggest beneficial kidney effects. Additionally, immune-related gene expression was down-regulated in males and up-regulated in females, further illustrating sex-specific responses. Pathway analyses showed enrichment in immune function, collagen degradation, extracellular matrix regulation, metabolism of vitamins and cofactors, pancreatic secretion, aging, and mitochondrial function in both sexes. Grapes provide resilience against fibrosis and related pathologies and protect renal health.

Multiorgan Effects of Grapes. Employing female mice, long-term supplementation with a semi-synthetic diet equivalent to the human consumption of 2.5 servings of grapes per day for 12 months led to significant gene expression changes across multiple organs, including the liver, kidney, colon, and ovary. Analysis of differentially expressed genes indicated that grape consumption may enhance metabolic detoxification and regulate reactive oxygen species in the liver, support cellular metabolism, and promote anti-inflammatory activity in the ovary and kidney. In the colon, the data suggested a role in suppressing mitochondrial dysfunction, reducing inflammation, and maintaining homeostasis. Pathway analysis revealed a combination of up- and down-regulation across tissues, with gene expression predominantly up-regulated in the kidney and down-regulated in the ovary. These findings suggest that grape consumption broadly influences gene expression throughout the body, potentially contributing to the diverse physiological benefits observed in tissues such as the brain, heart, eye, bladder, and colon.

Effect Grapes on Male and Female Liver and Colon. For these analyses, the overall experimental approach has been described previously. The results presented below are novel. In brief, to assess the impact of grape consumption on aging and metabolic regulation, male and female C57BL/6 J mice were introduced to a standard diet at 4 weeks of age. Upon reaching 1 year, mice were randomly assigned into four equal groups. Half of the mice continued on the standard diet, while the other half were switched to the grape diet, adding 5 percent grape powder to a mouse diet roughly equates to the human consumption of two servings of grapes per day. The study spanned 2.5 years, approximating 80 years of human age, allowing for an assessment of dietary impact over a full lifespan. At the conclusion of the study, mice from each group were euthanized following an overnight fasting period, and tissues, including the liver, colon, kidney, and skeletal muscles, were collected for analysis. R N A sequencing was employed to investigate the gene expression changes. As illustrated below, the findings revealed sex-specific differences in response to grape consumption across multiple tissues.

Effects of Grapes on Gene Expression in Male and Female Liver. In males, G O term biological process-related pathways showed enrichment of circadian rhythm, rhythmic processes, circadian behavior, and steroid metabolic process. In contrast, immune-related pathways such as adaptive immune response, granulocyte migration, positive regulation of immune response, cell activation involved in immune response, and immune response-activating signal transduction were down-regulated compared to the standard male diet. This suggests that the grape diet may regulate immune responses while promoting metabolic functions in the liver. In females, the G O term biologic process showed enrichment in immune-related pathways such as leukocyte migration, T cell proliferation, lymphocyte proliferation, mononuclear cell proliferation, leukocyte proliferation, positive regulation of T cell proliferation, together with pathways involved in metal ion homeostasis, ankyrin binding, tetrapyrrole biosynthesis and metabolism, erythrocyte development, porphyrin-containing compound metabolism, exogenous drug catabolism, and cell surface interactions at the vascular wall. These pathways are essential for maintaining liver homeostasis and highlight various mechanisms through which the grape diet enhances liver physiology.

A comparison of all four groups revealed that the majority of genes were expressed similarly. However, each of the four groups also demonstrated expression of genes unique to their cohort, indicating differences between males and females, with and without grape supplementation. When comparing the respective male and female groups, differential expression patterns were induced by supplementing the diet with grapes.

Effects of Grapes on Gene Expression in Male and Female Colon. In the colon, the grape diet affects gene expression in both sexes with greater uniformity, primarily influencing digestion, absorption, and metabolic processes. In males, the G O term biological process showed enrichment of digestion and absorption, fatty acid binding, quaternary ammonium group binding, metallocarboxypeptidase, and carboxypeptidase activity, multicellular organismal water homeostasis, lipid digestion, epidermis development, and zymogen granule formation. In females, indicated by G O term biological process, the same pathways were enriched, together with additional pathways linked to lipase activity, carboxylic ester hydrolase activity, and antimicrobial humoral response. Reactome analyses indicated pathways such as Cobalamin transport and metabolism, digestion and absorption, and digestion of dietary carbohydrate present in both males and females. As with the liver, a comparison of all four groups revealed that the majority of genes were expressed similarly, but each of the four groups also demonstrated unique expression as well, indicating differences between males and females, with and without grape supplementation. Also, as with the liver, when comparing the respective male and female groups, differential expression patterns were observed when the diets were supplemented with grapes. These results highlight the role of nutrigenomics in shaping dietary influences on gene expression and sex-specific physiological responses. While further research is needed to confirm these effects in humans, this study shows the potential of dietary interventions in modulating gene expression, promoting tissue health, and mitigating age-related functional decline.

Nutrigenomic Effects in Humans and Primates. In the current volume, chapters are included that describe the influence of grape consumption on the human microbiome as well as the human metabolome. In this section, we present information related to grapes and human nutrigenomics. It is now apparent that all of the subject areas, and more, are interrelated and need to be intertwined in a holistic manner in order to approach a realistic understanding of the human megaverse. Unfortunately, this is beyond our scope, but given this proposition, it is worth noting some examples of how diet influences the metabolome.

Manoharan and colleagues investigated the metabolome of human subjects provided with the Okinawan-based Nordic diet for 12 weeks and observed changes in the beta-diversity of the microbiome and changes in 323 of 602 metabolites. These diet-induced changes corresponded with reduced weight and inflammation and improved glucose and lipid metabolism. Many clinical investigations have reported metabolomic alterations following the consumption of the Mediterranean diet, with a variety of different subject groups, for example, cardiovascular risk, pregnancy, low physical activity, cognitive decline, all with promising outcomes.

Sample collection for metabolomic studies is rather straightforward: blood, urine, feces. For nutrigenomics, the situation is more complicated. While the question of greatest interest may relate to changes in gene expression in various tissues or organs, when studying the effects of diet in humans or primates, harvesting tissues for the purpose of analysis is not practicable. Accordingly, peripheral blood mononuclear cells have typically been used for transcriptomic profiling.

Focusing on key components of the Mediterranean diet using a curated database containing data from the peripheral blood mononuclear cells of human subjects, researchers reported changes in gene expression, suggesting the diet may be useful in reducing schizophrenia, neoplasms, rheumatic arthritis, and dementia in obese patients. Also employing peripheral blood mononuclear cells obtained from human subjects after weight loss secondary to a hypocaloric Mediterranean diet, researchers identified 634 differentially expressed genes of which 262 demonstrated relative higher expression levels and 372 demonstrated lower expression levels. They conclude these changes are consistent with modifying the endocrine system and nutritional disease.

With the cynomolgus macaque, using peripheral blood mononuclear cells for analysis, researchers investigated the effect of diet manipulations, Western or Mediterranean diet, over a period of 15 months. As a result, approximately 40 percent of the transcripts showed differential expression. Further, the Western diet shifted expression patterns to those associated with adiposity in humans. In addition, behavior changes were altered, with the Western diet leading to greater anxiety and reduced socialization.

Thus, it is clear that diet can have a strong influence on the microbiome, metabolome, and nutrigenomic responses. It is important to bear this in mind when studying the effects of grapes. When working with laboratory animals, responses can be closely linked to grape-mediated effects since isocaloric, semi-synthetic diets can be supplemented with grapes and used for control studies. When dealing with human subjects, some dietary restrictions can be recommended, but the influence of other dietary confounders must be borne in mind.

Now, turning to grapes, researchers performed a one-year clinical trial in which human subjects at risk for cardiovascular disease were provided with placebo, grape extract, or grape extract supplement with resveratrol. Subjects provided with the grape extract, with or without resveratrol supplementation, showed decreased high-sensitivity C-reactive protein, tumor necrosis factor-alpha, plasminogen activator inhibitor type 1, and interleukin-6 to interleukin-10 ratio, and increased anti-inflammatory interleukin-10. The results suggest dietary intervention as a means of primary prevention of cardiovascular disease.

However, it was subsequently reported that only the resveratrol-supplemented grape extract demonstrated an effect based on transcriptional profiling of inflammatory genes in peripheral blood mononuclear cells. Namely, six key inflammation-related transcription factors were significantly activated or inhibited, and 27 extracellular-space acting genes involved in inflammation, cell migration, and T-cell interaction signals were downregulated.

In a third study performed with hypertensive, diabetic male patients, only the grape extract supplemented with resveratrol mediated the investigated effects with peripheral blood mononuclear cells. The expression of the pro-inflammatory cytokines C C L 3, I L-1 beta, and T N F-alpha was reduced and transcriptional repressor L R R F I P-1 was increased. Also, a group of miRs involved in the regulation of the inflammatory response were modulated in the group consuming the resveratrol supplement grape extract for 12 months. Studies of direct relevance to the current discussion have been reported by Milella and colleagues. Peripheral blood mononuclear cells from human subjects were analyzed at time zero, at day 21 following a table grape-rich diet, and after a 30-day washout period. A total of 930 differentially expressed transcripts were identified. Functional interpretations suggested antithrombotic and anti-inflammatory effects on day 21 as well as day 30. Other favorable transcriptional changes were interpreted to be associated with immune response, D N A and protein repair, autophagy, and mitochondrial biogenesis. Gene functional analysis revealed changes suggestive of anti-thrombotic and anti-inflammatory effects.

In a second report by this group, healthy subjects ate black seedless grapes for three consecutive weeks. Following grape consumption, analysis of peripheral blood mononuclear cells demonstrated the modulation of 463 genes, and this number increased to 849 one month later. The investigators conclude that more than 200 of these genes are non-coding R N As that are important in regulating gene expression, and further studies revealed the modulation of 20 circulating micro R N As, mainly implicated in cancer development. Overall, the results indicated that grape intake exerts beneficial effects by modulating genes involved in critical physiological processes such as the immune response, inflammation, autophagy, D N A repair, and mitochondrial functionality.

It is clear from this work that human consumption of grapes modulates gene expression and thereby mediates a nutrigenomic response. As additional evidence, in a human tissue other than peripheral blood mononuclear cells, as part of a study examining the potential of grapes to diminish the response of human skin to ultraviolet irradiation, skin biopsies were procured and subjected to R N A-Seq analysis. The results of differential gene expression are shown with four different human subjects, designated as S, T, V, and X. Elements of the Venn diagrams labeled as S1, T1, V1, and X1 were derived from biopsies taken at the start of the protocol, following a two-week period on a bland, restricted diet. The second elements of the Venn diagrams, labeled as S2, T2, V2, and X2, are biopsies taken from the same subjects following two weeks of supplementing the bland, restricted diet with the equivalent of three servings of fresh grapes per day.

In every case, approximately 10 percent of the recorded gene transcripts are uniquely expressed in the individual skin specimens following grape consumption. This is compelling evidence that eating grapes mediates a nutrigenomic effect in human beings.

Future Directions. Until a time when human organs can readily be replaced with completely functional facsimiles, the current focus on healthspan, healthy living within lifespan, seems more reasonable than focusing on extending maximal life expectancy. Advancing precision nutrition and optimizing dietary interventions can certainly have an influence on healthspan.

Including grapes in the diet is already commonplace but potentially could be viewed as a dietary intervention that is broadly applicable to all age groups in the general population. If health benefits were to be proven, grape consumption may increase, but irrespective of this consideration, it is a value to know if and how grapes function. It is currently clear from clinical trials that grape consumption does have the potential of influencing human health. Since human clinical trials that measure clinical endpoints is the only way of definitively demonstrating efficacy, it would be desirable to conduct more trials with greater numbers of subjects in the future. This is certainly feasible given the safety of grape consumption and the availability of a surrogate standardized product that is fully representative of the whole food.

The ability of grapes to affect such a large variety of human ailments at first seems surprising. However, given the causative commonality, for example, oxidative stress and inflammation, and the capability of the grape to mediate a nutrigenomic response, the pleiotropic responses mediated by grapes are credible. We have shown multiorgan phenotypic changes induced by grape consumption in animal models consistent with health improvements. This is especially true with brain, liver, muscle, and kidney. Differences exist between males and females. While this work is very promising, it is realized that investigating the implications that can be gleaned from interrogating over 20,000 gene transcripts is only a start. It is reasonable to assume transcriptional modification leads downstream responses, but this needs to be shown in greater detail. Further, the influence of epigenetic markers such as D N A methylation and histone modifications, and other nutrigenetic factors, remains to be explored. And even beyond this, the interrelationships with other omic outcomes that are modulated by grape consumption, for example, metabolome and microbiome, should be investigated. This is a daunting task, but not beyond the capability of contemporary science.

Finally, it is important to note that work with grapes is not ethereal. We know that millions of tons of grapes are consumed by human beings every year, and we know that grapes affect the microbiome, the metabolome, and the gene expression of humans, as currently illustrated with peripheral blood mononuclear cells and skin. Although it remains to be shown, it is perfectly reasonable to expect modulation of gene expression in other tissues and organs of humans, and this will probably vary individual by individual and by sex. Not only are we learning about grape action, which may already have an ongoing yet ill-defined effect on human health, but grapes are serving as a prototype that is fueling the nutrigenomics era.
